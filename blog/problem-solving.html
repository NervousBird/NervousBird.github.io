Problem Solving in JS

Why the hell is it doing that?!

What this blog is gonna be about

I'm going to talk about my unfinished Music Machine "app" that I was creating using Tone.js. This was going to be a simple way to create music, using different instruments, it started out as just a drum machine, but grew a lot larger in scale, as tends to happen with these things. I eventually ended up with something that could play a basic piano, be affected by distortion, reverb, or other effects, and had a built in Bach Prelude piece that I had written.
Most importantly, it had a technical issue that I struggled with, and managed to fix, and can now use in future projects to save a lot of time! And that is: dropdown menus. Extremely common, extremely useful, and tedious to setup. But during my time making this app, I had to figure out a way to generate dropdown menus, and once I had this figured out, I could now generate dropdown menus anywhere, anytime.

The problem

Creating a music machine that lets you change instruments, effects, tempos and timings, and various other aspects means that you can't just create a static HTML framework and be done. Almost all the content created could be deleted, added to, and edited, which meant that it had to all be done via JS.



This meant that, not only did I need to create the elements and innerHTML for each and every dropdown, I had to create the content of each one. And to make matters worse, I had to make them all function!
This meant adding event listeners on the fly, updating them so they worked, and making sure that the dropdown menus closed when you clicked somewhere else!



How I fixed it

Lots of googling and asking LLM. I was on message boards a lot, I was writing psuedocode, and I was just trying things. I had to generate arrays in JS that would grab the elements I wanted in HTML, and apply the correct code to make the dropdown menu function. This meant a clever use of class names, that would universally apply, as well as navigating through parent and child elements.
This was code that I found googling, and from this experience, I really found that message boards were the best place to look! It's helpful to have a back and forth between people, as well as various solutions!



This will allow me to grab what ever I want to have dropdown functionality, and apply that. Simply need to input the ID of the button that will have the dropdown. But this isn't enough! I also want the menu to disappear if I click anywhere else. So we have to write a function to do that: Grab the event, grab the window, make a check to see if a 'click' action on the mouse is targetting the dropdown, or not!


Why Is it important to have various solutions?

In my opinion, this helps to understand what the problems are, and what the solutions might be. Problem solving for me, is:
What do I have?, What do I want to have?, How do I achieve that?
The first two seem simple, right? I have a menu, I want it to appear and disappear when certain things are clicked. But looking at how other people have answered the last question, can change the first two. I have a menu in HTML, well, maybe that's not what I want to have, maybe I want to have a menu in JS. Suddenly the simple question, What do I have? Isn't so simple.


Seeing others approach problems from the beginning helps you broaden your thinking, it's not just How do I get from A to B?, sometimes the A isn't even where you want to start.



The next problem...

We need content. The examples bellow are not from my music machine, but from this blog actually! But the Music Machine had the same issue: I want to be able to change the content easily. No HTML, the content needs to be easy to edit, and able to be created at will, I don't want to have to create HTML for ever possible situation, and then just hide and show.


The fix to this problem is actually something that the Kata challenges had you work on (if you did those), and brings me to my next, and maybe the most fun problem solving technique: DO.
Challenge sites, small silly apps, or active courses, are great ways to learn problem solving and solutions. There's a million ways to do things, and the only way to know which is best is by trying them.


We know how to google, we know how to ask questions. These are easy skills to acquire, LLM's exist (but they're stupid). The best way I have found to solve problems, is to just make things. You never know what solutions you will find when doing something.



I figured out these solutions while creating other things. In fact, I may not have even realised this was a solution if I hadn't been doing other things. I could have started at the wrong A. The chances are, that there's an even better solution out there, I may not be able to find it either by trying to problem solve this. I could, however, realise while solving another problem, that it also solves this one.



Is it easy?

Is problem solving easy? Well ... no, but yes. The lucky thing is, almost every problem you will come across has probably been solved before. There's a world out there, with billions of people, years of history, there are a lot of solved problems. The difficult bit is figuring out if the solution is the correct one for you.


Psuedocode, rubber ducky, error messages, console.log, google, asking others, even just trying things. I'm comfortable with them all, as I think anyone needs to be. I think the most important thing is to just, keep your mind open, and understand it fundamentally. Anyone of these methods could lead to a solution, but I don't think just finding a solution is truly good problem solving. It needs to be understood, regardless of context.


I can do it myself!! (I swear)

Referring to the above blurb, while you might find a solution through Google, Psuedocode, or the rubber ducky method, you may not actually understand it. Which is the truly important part. I know that I had lots of struggles with asking people for help, especially during my previous course, as it was all in Dutch. I felt uncomfortable with the communication, but letting that stop me was silly, and damaging. There was always a lot more to learn, and a lot more to understand, by just asking someone. In the future I will be actively trying to engage with others more, especially with problem solving. Who knows? Maybe their A is a better starting point.
